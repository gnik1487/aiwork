## shortContent
The internet used to be organized around information access. Today, it is organized around attention capture. Most major platforms are designed to maximize session time, interaction frequency, and ad relevance.

This creates a powerful business loop: more engagement produces more behavioral data; more data improves personalization; better personalization captures more attention. Over time, algorithms optimize for what keeps users active—not necessarily what informs them best.

For users, this has cognitive costs: fragmentation, impulsive switching, and reduced deep-focus windows. For society, it changes how news spreads, how narratives compete, and how collective priorities are formed.

The practical response is not digital abstinence but intentional design. At the individual level, this means defaults that protect focus: notification limits, friction before distraction, and content boundaries. At the platform level, it raises policy questions about transparency, accountability, and measurable well-being outcomes.
